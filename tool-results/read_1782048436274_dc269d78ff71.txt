    26→2. WHAT IS THIS?
    27→In a nutshell:
    28→Pericarditis simply means inflammation of the pericardium — the thin, two-layered sac that surrounds and protects the heart.
    29→
    30→Think of the pericardium like a soft balloon that the heart sits inside. Normally, the two layers glide smoothly against each other with a tiny amount of lubricating fluid between them. When they become inflamed, every heartbeat causes them to rub together like sandpaper, which produces the classic friction rub you hear on exam, and triggers severe pain.

    33→

    53→3. HOW DOES IT GO WRONG?
    54→This is where the exam clues get their “why”. Let's tell the story simply.
    55→
    56→Normal state:
    57→The pericardium has two layers — a tough outer sac (parietal) and a delicate inner layer stuck to the heart surface (visceral, or epicardium). Between them is a tiny film of fluid. No pain, no friction.
    58→
    59→Inflammation ignites:
    60→Whatever the trigger (virus, autoimmune disease, etc.), inflammatory cells invade both layers. Fibrin is deposited on their surfaces — like scrambled eggs coating a frying pan. Now, instead of gliding silently, the two layers scrape against each other with every heartbeat.
    61→
    62→How this creates your exam clues:
    63→
    64→Sharp, pleuritic, positional pain:
    65→The parietal pericardium (outer layer) is loaded with pain nerve fibers. When it's inflamed, every movement of the heart or lungs tugs and stretches it.
    66→
    67→Why lying down hurts most: Lying flat lets the heart fall back and stretch the inflamed pericardium further. Sitting forward reduces that pull.
    68→
    69→Why deep breath worsens it: The lungs expand and rub against the already angry outer pericardium.
    70→
    71→Friction rub:
    72→That scratchy, leather-on-leather sound you auscultate is literally the two roughened layers grating against each other. It's often a “to-and-fro” (systole and diastole) sound, best heard with the patient leaning forward and breath held. This rub is transient; once fluid accumulates between the layers (pericardial effusion), the rub fades away.
    73→
    74→ECG changes — the “superficial heart attack” mimic:
    75→The inflammation does not make a neat coronary-artery territory pattern. It bathes the heart's surface diffusely, irritating the underlying epicardium (the visceral layer).
    76→
    77→Diffuse ST elevation (concave-up): This is injury current from the inflamed epicardium. It's widespread (I, II, III, aVF, V2-V6), not localized like a STEMI.
    78→
    79→PR depression: The atria are thin-walled and their outer surface lies just beneath the pericardium, so atrial injury shows as PR segment depression in most leads. In lead aVR, you see reciprocal PR elevation.
    80→
    81→No reciprocal ST depression: Because the injury isn't a full-thickness regional infarct, you typically won't see reciprocal ST depression (except perhaps in aVR and V1, which are electrically “opposite”). This is a huge pattern-recognition differentiator from STEMI.
    82→
    83→What if fluid builds up? (Pericardial effusion):
    84→Intense inflammation makes the pericardium ooze fluid. This separates the two layers — the rub may vanish, and if enough fluid accumulates, it can squeeze the heart → cardiac tamponade (we'll discuss that later). The ECG might then show low QRS voltages and electrical alternans (QRS amplitude varying beat-to-beat as the heart swings in fluid).
    85→
    86→So in a stem, the pathophysiology you're “seeing” is:
    87→
    88→Inflamed pericardium → positional pain + rub
    89→
    90→Superficial epicardial injury → diffuse ST elevation + PR depression
    91→
    92→Possible effusion → muffled heart sounds, low voltages
    93→
    94→Everything connects directly to what you’ll read in a vignette.

   103→4. WHAT TRIGGERS IT?
   104→In your exam, the cause is rarely mysterious — they'll embed a clue in the patient's story. I want you to think of causes in bins, because the bin tells you the management and the prognosis.
   105→
   106→Here are the main trigger groups, ordered by how often you'll see them in MCQs.
   107→
   108→1. Idiopathic / Viral (80–90% of cases in the exam)
   109→
   110→Stem clues: Young, otherwise healthy person. Often a recent upper respiratory or gastrointestinal illness 1–2 weeks before. Fever and myalgias may be present.
   111→
   112→Pathology: Presumed viral, but often no specific virus is found. Common culprits: Coxsackie B, echovirus, adenovirus, influenza, EBV, CMV, parvovirus B19.
   113→
   114→Exam takeaway: If the stem says “flu-like illness last week” + the classic chest pain → this is your default.
   115→
   116→Prognosis: Usually self-limiting, resolves in 1–3 weeks with NSAIDs. But 15–30% may recur.
   117→
   118→2. Autoimmune / Systemic inflammatory
   119→
   120→Stem clues: The stem mentions known SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, systemic sclerosis, Sjögren's, or sarcoidosis. In lupus, pericarditis is the most common cardiac manifestation.
   121→
   122→Key link: A young woman with malar rash, arthritis, and sharp chest pain with diffuse ST elevation? Think lupus pericarditis.
   123→
   124→Management: Treat the underlying disease. Anti-inflammatory drugs; in severe/recurrent cases, colchicine, steroids, or biologic agents.
   125→
   126→3. Post-Myocardial Infarction (MI) — two distinct syndromes
   127→
   128→(a) Early post-MI pericarditis (“epistenocardiac pericarditis”):
   129→
   130→Timing: Within the first 1–5 days after an acute MI.
   131→
   132→Mechanism: Inflammation directly over the transmural infarct. It's localized.
   133→
   134→Stem: Patient with recent STEMI, now has recurrence of pleuritic chest pain, friction rub. ECG may show persistent ST elevation (but might be confounded by the infarct pattern).
   135→
   136→Key: This is a marker of a transmural infarct; treat the pain with NSAIDs but avoid if possible because they impair myocardial healing. Usually manage with aspirin, colchicine.
   137→
   138→(b) Late post-MI pericarditis (Dressler syndrome):
   139→
   140→Timing: 2–10 weeks after an MI (or cardiac surgery).
   141→
   142→Mechanism: Autoimmune reaction to exposed myocardial antigens.
   143→
   144→Stem: Patient had an MI weeks ago, now presents with fever, pericarditic pain, friction rub, elevated ESR/CRP, and sometimes pleural effusion/pneumonitis.
   145→
   146→Key: Treat with NSAIDs, colchicine; steroids if refractory.
   147→
   148→Exam twist: They may also call it “post-cardiac injury syndrome” (after cardiac surgery, trauma, or pulmonary embolism). So same story: recent heart surgery → weeks later → pleuritic pain + fever + rub = post-pericardiotomy syndrome.
   149→
   150→4. Bacterial (Purulent pericarditis)
   151→
   152→Stem clues: Very sick patient, high fevers, rigors, possibly septic. Often an adjacent infection: pneumonia, empyema, or after thoracic surgery / trauma. Can occur in immunocompromised (e.g., HIV, transplant).
   153→
   154→Organisms: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae.
   155→
   156→Exam red flag: They'll describe a rapidly deteriorating patient with large effusion and tamponade. Requires emergent drainage + IV antibiotics. Mortality very high if missed.
   157→
   158→5. Tuberculous pericarditis
   159→
   160→Stem clues: Subacute to chronic onset (weeks–months) of constitutional symptoms: low-grade fever, night sweats, weight loss, fatigue. Often in a patient from an endemic area, or with known HIV. Large pericardial effusion is common, often hemorrhagic.
   161→
   162→Consequence: High risk for developing constrictive pericarditis later.
   163→
   164→Key exam test: Pericardial fluid: exudative, lymphocyte-predominant, elevated adenosine deaminase (ADA). Culture/PCR for TB. Treatment: anti-tuberculous therapy + corticosteroids (to reduce constriction risk).
   165→
   166→6. Uremic pericarditis
   167→
   168→Stem: Patient with end-stage renal disease (ESRD), on dialysis or not. Often presents with pericarditic pain, but may be silent.
   169→
   170→Mechanism: Uremic toxins irritate the pericardium.
   171→
   172→Management: Intensify dialysis. If recurrent, consider pericardiocentesis or pericardial window. NSAIDs may be used but with caution due to bleeding risk.
   173→
   174→7. Malignant pericarditis
   175→
   176→Stem: Known lung cancer, breast cancer, lymphoma, or melanoma. Insidious onset of dyspnoea, chest pain, signs of effusion/tamponade.
   177→
   178→Fluid: Often bloody. Cytology for malignant cells.
   179→
   180→Management: Treat underlying malignancy, pericardiocentesis, or window.
   181→
   182→8. Radiation-induced pericarditis
   183→
   184→Stem: History of mediastinal radiation (e.g., for Hodgkin lymphoma, breast cancer) many years earlier. May present as acute pericarditis or chronic constrictive pericarditis.

   234→5. WHAT DOES IT LOOK LIKE? — EXAM STEM PATTERNS
   235→You’ll never miss a pericarditis stem if you know the classic two‑minute story. But exams love to dress it up with distracting details. Let’s break down how it really shows up in a vignette.
   236→
   237→A. The Classic Presentation — the “Textbook” Stem
   238→They’ll hand you a patient (often young, 20–50 years old) with:
   239→
   240→1. Chest pain — the single most testable feature
   241→
   242→Quality: Sharp, stabbing, “like a knife.” Pleuritic (worsens with deep breathing, coughing, or lying flat).
   243→
   244→Positional magic: Pain is relieved by sitting up and leaning forward. This is the buzzword that unlocks the diagnosis.
   245→
   246→In the stem they might phrase it as: “He finds it most comfortable to sit on the edge of the bed and lean forward.” or “Pain worse when supine, better when upright.”
   247→
   248→Radiation: Typically to the trapezius ridge (neck/shoulder), because the phrenic nerve (which innervates the pericardium) originates from C3–C5. Not down the arm like MI.
   249→
   250→Duration: Often constant, lasting hours to days, with a gradual onset.
   251→
   252→2. Pericardial friction rub — the physical exam buzzword
   253→
   254→A scratchy, leather-like, to‑and‑fro sound (sometimes with three components: atrial systole, ventricular systole, ventricular diastole).
   255→
   256→Best heard with the diaphragm of the stethoscope at the left lower sternal border, with the patient sitting forward and holding their breath.
   257→
   258→If you see “scratchy sound synchronous with heartbeat” plus the pain story, it’s pathognomonic.
   259→
   260→3. ECG — the “diffuse” pattern
   261→We already touched on this, but for pattern recognition, memorize:
   262→
   263→Diffuse, concave-up (saddle-shaped) ST elevation in leads I, II, III, aVF, V2–V6. Not a territorial pattern (not just inferior, anterior, lateral).
   264→
   265→PR depression in most leads (very specific) and PR elevation in aVR.
   266→
   267→No reciprocal ST depression (except sometimes in aVR and V1).
   268→
   269→If they show you an ECG and ask “What is the cause?”, look for PR depression as a telltale sign.
   270→
   271→4. Prodrome and systemic features
   272→
   273→History of a “flu‑like illness” or URI 1–3 weeks earlier.
   274→
   275→Low‑grade fever, malaise, myalgias.
   276→
   277→Example classic stem:
   278→A 24‑year‑old man presents with 2 days of sharp central chest pain that is worse when he lies flat and takes a deep breath. He finds relief sitting forward. He had a cold 10 days ago. On exam, there is a scratchy sound at the left lower sternal border. ECG shows diffuse saddle‑shaped ST elevation and PR depression.
   279→Diagnosis? Pericarditis. End of story.
   280→
   281→B. Atypical Presentations — How the Exam Will Trick You
   282→1. The “Silent” or Vague Pericarditis
   283→
   284→Elderly patients, immunocompromised, or post‑surgical patients may not report typical chest pain. They may present with only dyspnoea, fatigue, or low‑grade fever.
   285→
   286→The stem may just describe a pericardial effusion found on echo, with no pain. Think of TB or malignancy in such subacute settings.
   287→
   288→2. Pain That Mimics MI (Myopericarditis)
   289→
   290→If the inflammation extends a little deeper into the underlying myocardium (myopericarditis), the troponin can be mildly elevated.
   291→
   292→The exam will give you a young person with diffuse ST elevation, chest pain, and a troponin of 0.5 ng/mL (slightly elevated). An inexperienced student might activate the cath lab. But the pain is positional, the ST changes are diffuse, and there are no wall motion abnormalities on echo.
   293→
   294→Key: In pure STEMI, troponin usually rises much higher and there are regional wall motion abnormalities.
   295→
   296→3. The “Post‑MI” Red Herring
   297→
   298→They’ll give you a 65‑year‑old man with ST‑elevation MI a week ago. Now he has recurrent chest pain and a friction rub. The ECG already has Q waves and ST elevation from the original infarct; how do you know it’s pericarditis?
   299→
   300→Look for new pleuritic/positional pain + rub. The ECG may not help, but the exam will signal with the rub and the positional nature. And they'll ask: “What is the most likely cause?” → Early post‑MI pericarditis. Or a few weeks later → Dressler syndrome.
   301→
   302→4. Tamponade Masquerading as Heart Failure
   303→
   304→A stem might skip the chest pain and go straight to a patient with dyspnoea, hypotension, muffled heart sounds, and distended neck veins (Beck’s triad). They want you to recognize that a large pericardial effusion (from pericarditis of any cause) led to tamponade.
   305→
   306→We’ll cover this in complications, but if you see “dyspnoea + hypotension + clear lungs”, think cardiac tamponade, not heart failure.

   323→6. WHAT DO YOU ORDER?

   325→
   326→First, a crucial framework: ESC Guidelines (2015) state you can diagnose acute pericarditis if a patient has at least 2 of these 4 criteria:
   327→
   328→Chest pain typical of pericarditis (sharp, pleuritic, improved by sitting forward)
   329→
   330→Pericardial friction rub on auscultation
   331→
   332→ECG changes – new widespread ST elevation or PR depression
   333→
   334→Pericardial effusion (new or worsening on imaging)
   335→
   336→In most classic exam stems, the patient gives you numbers 1, 2, and 3 — and that’s enough. You don’t even need an echo to diagnose; it’s a clinical + ECG diagnosis. But the exam will still test you on what you should order, and what the results mean.
   337→
   338→1. ECG — The King of First Steps
   339→
   340→When to order: Immediately, in any chest pain presentation.
   341→
   342→What you’ll see (and how to spot it in a tracing):
   343→
   344→Stage 1 (first hours–days): Diffuse, concave-up (saddle-shaped) ST elevation in I, II, III, aVF, V2–V6. PR depression in most leads, PR elevation in aVR. No reciprocal ST depression except aVR/V1.
   345→
   346→Stage 2 (days later): ST segments normalize, T waves flatten.
   347→
   348→Stage 3: T-wave inversion (diffuse, not deep and symmetrical like ischemia, but shallow).
   349→
   350→Stage 4: Everything normalizes, or T-wave inversions persist.
   351→
   352→Exam hack: They might show you a Stage 1 ECG and ask, “What’s the most likely diagnosis?” The PR depression is the secret handshake of pericarditis. If you see it, think pericardial inflammation.
   353→
   354→Critical distinction: If ST elevation is in a regional pattern (e.g., inferior leads only) with reciprocal depression in lateral leads → STEMI, not pericarditis.
   355→
   356→2. Blood Tests
   357→
   358→Inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP): Usually elevated. Not diagnostic alone, but support the inflammation story and help monitor response to treatment. The exam may ask, “Which lab value best monitors disease activity?” → CRP.
   359→
   360→Troponin (hs-cTn): Can be mildly elevated if there’s some myocardial involvement (myopericarditis). The stem will often report a “slightly raised troponin” to tempt you into a STEMI diagnosis.

   429→7. HOW DO YOU MANAGE IT?
   430→This is where the exam shifts from diagnosis to treatment decisions. Most cases are straightforward, but MCQs love testing your knowledge of first-line therapy, duration, when to hospitalize, and complication management.

   433→
   434→A. Acute Management — The “Uncomplicated” Pericarditis
   435→The typical patient is young, has classic pain, no effusion or only a small one, and no high-risk features. The exam wants you to pick a three-drug combo for effective first-line treatment:
   436→
   437→1. NSAIDs — The Pain & Inflammation Eraser
   438→
   439→First choice: Ibuprofen (600–800 mg three times daily) or Aspirin (750–1000 mg three times daily).
   440→
   441→Why: Rapid pain control, anti-inflammatory. Aspirin is preferred in post‑MI pericarditis (Dressler) because other NSAIDs can impair myocardial healing.
   442→
   443→Duration: Until pain resolves + CRP normalizes, typically 1–2 weeks. Don’t just stop abruptly; taper slowly to prevent recurrence (reduce dose by a quarter every 1–2 weeks).
   444→
   445→Exam catch: They might ask, “Which NSAID is contraindicated?” → Indomethacin is avoided in elderly and in post‑MI pericarditis (reduces coronary blood flow). So if you see indomethacin in a post‑MI stem, it’s the wrong answer.
   446→
   447→2. Colchicine — The Recurrence Preventer
   448→
   449→First-line alongside NSAIDs. The single biggest exam “next best step” in treatment.
   450→
   451→Dose: 0.5–1.0 mg once daily (weight-adjusted; <70 kg or renal impairment → 0.5 mg).
   452→
   453→Duration: 3 months. Not just during pain — it’s the full 3 months that halves recurrence rates.
   454→
   455→Mechanism: Inhibits microtubule assembly → disrupts inflammasome activation, cytokine release, and neutrophil chemotaxis.
   456→
   457→Side effects they’ll test: Gastrointestinal intolerance (diarrhea, nausea), dose-dependent. Rarely, bone marrow suppression, myotoxicity.
   458→
   459→Avoid or adjust in: Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) — dose reduction needed. Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, ketoconazole) increase toxicity.
   460→
   461→Exam tip: If the stem says “despite NSAIDs, pain persists or recurs in a few weeks” → answer is often “add colchicine” (if not already on it) or “ensure adequate duration (3 months).”
   462→
   463→3. Gastroprotection — The Ulcer Shield
   464→
   465→Always co-prescribe a proton pump inhibitor (e.g., pantoprazole 20–40 mg daily) with NSAIDs, especially if there’s any GI risk history.
   466→
   467→Exam point: An MCQ might list “What would you prescribe alongside aspirin?” → PPI.
   468→
   469→4. Rest and Activity Restriction
   470→
   471→Advise rest until symptom resolution + CRP normalization (usually 1–2 weeks).
   472→
   473→Athletes: No competitive sport for 3 months (until ECG + echo + inflammatory markers normal). This is a specific exam question: “When can an athlete with pericarditis return to play?” → At least 3 months, and only if fully asymptomatic, normal CRP, and no effusion/tamponade.

   605→8. WHAT IF YOU MISS IT? — COMPLICATIONS IN A LATE-PRESENTATION STEM
   606→The exam loves to test what happens if pericarditis isn’t recognized, treated early, or keeps smoldering. The three big ones you must recognise at a glance are cardiac tamponade, constrictive pericarditis, and recurrent pericarditis.
   607→
   608→A. Pericardial Effusion Leading to Cardiac Tamponade
   609→The story: Inflammation makes the pericardium leak fluid. If fluid accumulates slowly, the pericardium stretches and you can have a large effusion with minimal symptoms. But if it accumulates rapidly (or reaches the stretch limit), the pressure inside the sac compresses the heart chambers, preventing them from filling. Cardiac output drops drastically.
   610→
   611→Exam stem clues for effusion:
   612→
   613→The classic pericarditis pain and rub may decrease or disappear (the fluid separates the rubbing layers).
   614→
   615→ECG shows low QRS voltage (all complexes are small) and electrical alternans (QRS amplitude alternates every other beat — the heart swinging in the fluid).
   616→
   617→Chest X‑ray: massive, globular “water bottle” heart with clear lung fields.
   618→
   619→When tamponade supervenes — the stem you cannot miss:
   620→
   621→Beck’s triad: Hypotension + muffled heart sounds + distended neck veins (JVP raised).
   622→
   623→Pulsus paradoxus: Drop in systolic BP >10 mmHg during normal inspiration. The stem may say, “BP 90/60 mmHg; during inspiration, systolic BP falls to 78 mmHg.”
   624→
   625→Clear lungs on exam: (unlike cardiogenic shock from pump failure) — this is your key differentiator from acute heart failure.
   626→
   627→Echo: Right atrial collapse during systole, right ventricular diastolic collapse, plethoric IVC with blunted respiratory variation, exaggerated respiratory variation in mitral inflow (E wave variation >25%).
   628→
   629→MCQ pattern recognition:
   630→
   631→“A 45-year-old woman with lupus presents with 3 days of dyspnoea, hypotension, and clear lung fields. Her JVP is elevated to the earlobe, and BP drops sharply when she breathes in. What is the immediate next step?” → Emergent pericardiocentesis.
   632→
   633→They might try to fool you by listing “insert central line,” “start inotropes,” “CT chest.” The correct answer is always immediate echo‑guided pericardiocentesis (or blind subxiphoid if no echo). Fluids can be given temporarily as a bridge to increase preload.
   634→
   635→B. Constrictive Pericarditis — The Chronic Thief
   636→The story: If pericarditis becomes chronic (especially TB, radiation, prior cardiac surgery, recurrent autoimmune), the pericardium thickens and calcifies, forming a rigid shell around the heart. The ventricles can fill only in early diastole, then abruptly stop — the heart can’t relax fully. The result mimics right‑sided heart failure.
   637→
   638→How the stem will present it:
   639→
   640→Chronic: Weeks to months of dyspnoea, fatigue, abdominal swelling, pedal edema.
   641→
   642→Examination:
   643→
   644→Elevated JVP, often with a prominent “y” descent (Friedreich’s sign) and a steep, quick filling that collapses — Kussmaul’s sign (JVP rises on inspiration, not falls, because the rigid pericardium prevents the right heart from accepting more venous return).
   645→
   646→Pericardial knock: A loud early diastolic sound, heard best at the apex or lower left sternal border, resembling a third heart sound but sharper and earlier.
   647→
   648→Ascites and hepatomegaly out of proportion to peripheral edema — a huge clue.
   649→
   650→Pulsatile hepatomegaly (rarely tested but specific).
   651→
   652→ECG: Low voltage, atrial fibrillation (common), diffuse T‑wave inversions (chronic pericarditis).
   653→
   654→Imaging: Chest X‑ray may show pericardial calcification (best on lateral view). CT/MRI confirms thickened pericardium (>4 mm) and calcifications. Echo shows septal bounce, preserved/increased mitral annulus velocity (e′ >8 cm/s) — opposite to restrictive cardiomyopathy where e′ is reduced. This is the classic way the exam distinguishes constrictive pericarditis from restrictive cardiomyopathy on echo.
   655→
   656→Cardiac catheterization: “Square root sign” or “dip and plateau” pattern in ventricular diastolic pressures, with equalization of end‑diastolic pressures in all four chambers.
   657→
   658→Management: Pericardiectomy (surgical stripping of the pericardium). Diuretics for symptom relief while awaiting surgery. Treat underlying cause if active (TB).
   659→
   660→Exam stem snippet:
   661→“A 58-year-old man with a history of Hodgkin lymphoma treated with chest radiation 20 years ago presents with progressive ascites and pedal edema. JVP is elevated with a prominent ‘y’ descent and rises further on inspiration. Echocardiogram shows a septal bounce and a thickened pericardium. Mitral annular tissue Doppler velocity (e′) is 12 cm/s.” → Constrictive pericarditis.
   662→
   663→C. Myopericarditis Complications
   664→Inflammation may extend into the underlying myocardium, causing myopericarditis.
   665→
   666→Stem: The usual pericarditis picture plus mild troponin elevation, and sometimes reduced LV systolic function on echo.
   667→
   668→Complications: Ventricular arrhythmias, acute heart failure, chronic cardiomyopathy.
   669→
   670→Management: Restrict physical activity strictly for 3–6 months. Hospital admission. Standard pericarditis treatment, but high‑dose NSAIDs used cautiously (debated). Colchicine still recommended.
   671→
   672→D. Recurrent Pericarditis
   673→Stem: Patient had a first episode of pericarditis months ago, now the same symptoms return. May have been treated initially without colchicine or with a short course.
   674→
   675→Risk factors: Inadequate initial therapy, premature cessation of NSAIDs/colchicine, autoimmune disease.
   676→
   677→Management: Re‑start NSAIDs + colchicine (full 3‑month course). If colchicine‑dependent or NSAID‑dependent, consider IL‑1 inhibitors (anakinra).
   678→
   679→Exam hint: They might say “treated with ibuprofen for 1 week, symptoms resolved, now recurrence at 3 weeks.” The mistake was no colchicine and tapering too quickly. Next step: Add colchicine + NSAID + PPI and ensure taper.
