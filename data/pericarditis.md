## Pericarditis
**One-liner:** Inflammation of pericardium; typically viral/post-infectious in young adults; risk of tamponade and constriction if missed.

### Presentation
- **Cardinal symptoms:** Sharp, pleuritic chest pain (worse lying flat, better sitting forward), may radiate to trapezius ridge; fever, malaise, dyspnoea; often prodrome of URTI or GI symptoms.
- **Key exam findings:** Pericardial rub (scratchy, triphasic, transient; best heard with diaphragm at left lower sternal border, patient leaning forward); tachycardia; pulsus paradoxus if effusion present; muffled heart sounds if large effusion; signs of systemic infection.
- **Red flags that change urgency:** Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, raised JVP, pulsus paradoxus) → suspect tamponade; high fever >39°C, immunosuppression → consider purulent/tuberculous pericarditis; rapid onset of large effusion; recent MI → post-MI pericarditis/Dressler’s; chest trauma/instrumentation.

### Differential Diagnosis
- **Most likely -> less likely, with 1-line differentiators:**
  - **Acute coronary syndrome (ACS):** Pain not pleuritic, not positional, troponin rise proportional to ischaemia; ECG: ST elevation concave vs. pericarditis diffuse.
  - **Pulmonary embolism:** Pleuritic pain + dyspnoea, ECG may show S1Q3T3, right heart strain; D-dimer, CTPA.
  - **Pneumonia:** Focal fever, productive cough, consolidation on CXR; pleuritic pain but localised.
  - **Myocarditis:** Often younger, viral prodrome, heart failure symptoms, arrhythmias; troponin high, normal coronaries on angiography.
  - **Aortic dissection:** Tearing pain, radiating to back, pulse deficit, widened mediastinum on CXR.
  - **Costochondritis:** Tender chest wall on palpation, no fever, normal ECG/echo.

### Investigations
- **Bedside:** ECG (as soon as possible) – look for diffuse concave ST elevation (≥0.5mm in limb leads, ≥1mm in chest leads), PR depression (especially in II, aVF, V4-V6); may see electrical alternans if large effusion; tachycardia; blood pressure + pulsus paradoxus (if uncertain, measure with BP cuff: drop in SBP >10mmHg on inspiration is abnormal); pulse oximetry.
- **Bloods:** FBC (WCC, CRP – elevated in infectious/inflammatory); troponin (may be mildly elevated – differentiate from MI); ESR; viral serology if suspected (Coxsackie, EBV, influenza); blood cultures if febrile; ANA, dsDNA, TB workup if recurrent/atypical.
- **Imaging:** **First-line:** CXR (enlarged cardiac silhouette if >300ml effusion – “water-bottle” shape); **Second-line:** Echocardiogram (transthoracic) – diagnostic for effusion, assess size/compression; look for diastolic collapse of RA/RV. **CT chest** if suspected purulent/complicated; **Cardiac MRI** for chronic/constrictive pericarditis (shows pericardial thickening, oedema, inflammation).
- **Special tests:** Pericardial fluid analysis (if drainage indicated): check cytology, culture, ADA (TB), Gram stain.
- **Diagnostic criteria / scoring system:** **EuroPep criteria** (clinical diagnosis): 2 of: (1) typical chest pain, (2) pericardial rub, (3) ECG changes (diffuse ST elevation or PR depression), (4) new or worsening pericardial effusion. If only 1, need CRP elevation or imaging evidence of inflammation.

### Management
- **Immediate (first hour / ED):** Assess haemodynamics. If suspected **cardiac tamponade**: call cardiology/CCU, prepare for urgent pericardiocentesis; give IV fluids if hypotensive (500ml bolus, cautious). If stable: **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen 400-800mg PO TDS or aspirin 750mg-1g QDS) unless contraindicated. **Colchicine** 0.5mg BD (add on for recurrence, also first line in acute with NSAID). Pain control – avoid heavy opioids; they suppress respiration and may mask worsening.
- **Ward (first 24-72h):** Monitor for tamponade (vital signs, JVP, pulsus paradoxus q4h). Repeat echo if effusion enlarging or symptoms change. Titrate NSAIDs to pain relief. Add PPI if NSAID (e.g., lansoprazole 15-30mg OD). Start colchicine (if not started) – reduces recurrence by 50%. Consider **prednisolone** 0.25-0.5mg/kg/day *only* if resistant to NSAID/colchicine or if autoimmune cause (caution: steroids increase recurrence risk if used early in viral pericarditis). Check renal function before NSAIDs.
- **Long-term / outpatient:** Taper NSAIDs over 2-4 weeks. Continue colchicine for 3 months (up to 6 months for second episode). Lifestyle: avoid strenuous activity until pain free and normal CRP (usually 2-4 weeks). Recurrent pericarditis: consider colchicine for 6-12 months, IL-1 antagonists (anakinra) in refractory cases. Refer to rheumatology if autoimmune/autoinflammatory. Follow-up echo at 4 weeks to confirm resolution.

### Drugs & Doses
- **First-line agents:**
  - **Ibuprofen:** 400-800mg PO TDS (max 2.4g/day) – preferred for better GI tolerability and less coronary vasoconstriction than indomethacin.
  - **Aspirin:** 750mg-1g PO QDS (max 4g/day) – use if post-MI or known CAD.
  - **Colchicine:** 0.5mg PO BD (0.5mg OD if <70kg or elderly). Start with NSAID. PPI cover if on NSAID.
- **Common alternatives:**
  - **Indomethacin:** 25-50mg PO TDS (second-line; GI side effects).
  - **Prednisolone:** 0.25-0.5mg/kg/day PO – taper over 2-4 weeks after symptom control. Use only if contraindication to NSAID/colchicine (e.g., renal failure, allergy) or failure after 1 week.
- **Key contraindications / interactions:** NSAIDs avoid in eGFR<30, active GI bleed, breastfeeding (use ibuprofen low dose), aspirin allergy. Colchicine: reduce dose with renal impairment; CI with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., clarithromycin, ketoconazole). Steroids: avoid before confirming no infection (TB, fungal). Pain-control NSAIDs + colchicine: risk of diarrhoea (colchicine) – counsel patient.

### Complications
- **Acute:** **Cardiac tamponade** (hypotension, JVP raised, muffled heart sounds – Beck’s triad; pulsus paradoxus, Kussmaul’s sign). **Purulent pericarditis** (high fever, sepsis, rapid effusion). **Constrictive pericarditis** (rare acute; more common in TB).
- **Chronic:** **Recurrent pericarditis** (20-30% risk; higher if colchicine not used or steroid used early). **Constrictive pericarditis** (develops weeks to months; signs: heart failure, Kussmaul’s, pericardial knock on echo). **Chronic pericardial effusion** – may cause compressive symptoms.

### Prognosis
- **Mortality <1%** in viral/idiopathic; excellent recovery with treatment. ~10-15% develop recurrent episodes. Constrictive pericarditis (5%) requires surgical pericardiectomy if symptoms persist. Purulent pericarditis has high mortality (20-40%) – requires aggressive drainage and antibiotics.

### Pearls & Pitfalls
- **ECG PR depression is the most specific sign** – often missed; look for it in inferior/lateral leads; ST elevation concave (not convex like STEMI).
- **Use the 4-finger rule:** If ST elevation present, check if it’s in multiple territories (pericarditis) vs. one (STEMI).
- **Don’t start steroids early in viral pericarditis** – they increase recurrence risk. If patient already on steroids for another reason, do not stop abruptly; instead, slow taper.
- **Colchicine + NSAID for all first episodes** unless contraindicated – reduces recurrence dramatically.
- **Pitfall: blaming pleuritic pain on “musculoskeletal”** without ECG; especially in young adults. A normal CRP does not rule out pericarditis – repeat echo if suspicion remains.
- **Pitfall: missing tamponade** – tachycardia and hypotension are late signs; look for pulsus paradoxus (drop >10mmHg). If unsure, ask senior for echo urgently.

### Escalation Criteria
- **Call cardiology / CCU immediately if:**
  - Suspected **tamponade** (pulsus paradoxus >20mmHg, hypotension, raised JVP, electrical alternans).
  - New large effusion on echo with any haemodynamic compromise.
  - Purulent appearance of fluid on pericardiocentesis.
- **Call senior if:**
  - Persistent pain after 48h of NSAID/colchicine.
  - Fever >38.5°C for >72h (exclude bacterial/TB).
  - Rising inflammatory markers on treatment.
  - Recurrent effusion after initial drainage.
- **Call ICU if:**
  - Shock (MAP<65) not responsive to fluids.
  - Need for vasopressor support or mechanical ventilation.
  - Pericardiocentesis with complications (myocardial puncture, haemorrhage).
- **Specific thresholds:** **NEWS2 ≥5** (especially if dropping trend), **lactate >2 mmol/L**, **GCS<15**. Persistent tachycardia >100 despite pain control. Any systolic BP <90 or drop >20mmHg on inspiration.
