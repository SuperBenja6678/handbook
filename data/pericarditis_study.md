## Pericarditis
**One-liner:** Inflammation of the pericardium causing sharp retrosternal chest pain, ECG changes, and risk of effusion/tamponade; most often viral/idiopathic in young adults.

### 1. Pattern Recognition — The Patient You'll See
- **Classic vignette:** A 32-year-old man presents to the acute medical unit with sudden-onset sharp, central chest pain that is worse when he lies flat and better when he sits up and leans forward. He had a "cold" last week with mild fever and sore throat. Pain is pleuritic — hurts when he takes a deep breath. He looks uncomfortable, takes shallow breaths, and his pulse is 100, BP 110/70, temperature 37.8°C. On auscultation, you hear a scratchy sound over the left sternal border. ECG shows diffuse ST elevation in multiple leads.

- **Dissect the vignette:**
  - **Sharp, central chest pain** – Pericarditis pain is typically sharp (not crushing), retrosternal, and radiates to the trapezius ridge (C3-5 phrenic nerve referral).
  - **Worse supine, better leaning forward** – Classic positional nature; inflamed pericardium contacts chest wall more when supine.
  - **Recent URI** – Most common trigger (viral aetiology).
  - **Pleuritic component** – Inflammation irritates adjacent pleura.
  - **Scratchy sound (pericardial rub)** – Friction between inflamed layers; pathognomonic.
  - **Diffuse ST elevation on ECG** – Unlike MI, the ST elevation is concave upwards and not localising to a single vascular territory.

- **Atypical presentations that may trick you:**
  1. **Silent effusion/tamponade:** No pain, but dyspnoea, tachycardia, hypotension, and muffled heart sounds (Beck’s triad). Often post-cardiac surgery or in malignancy.
  2. **Uraemic pericarditis:** Dialysis patient with chest pain, but ECG may show diffuse ST elevation with tall tented T waves (hyperkalaemia). Rub is still present.
  3. **Tuberculous pericarditis:** Subacute course (weeks), night sweats, weight loss, large effusion with high protein/LDH. May present with constrictive physiology without typical pain.

### 2. What Is This? (Definition & Why It Matters)
Pericarditis is inflammation of the pericardium (the fibroelastic sac surrounding the heart). It can be acute (<4 weeks), incessant, recurrent, or chronic. The inflammation causes pain, ECG changes, and can lead to pericardial effusion (fluid accumulation) and, rarely, cardiac tamponade (haemodynamic compromise from compression) or constrictive pericarditis (chronic fibrosis that restricts filling).

Why you must know this topic well:  
- It accounts for ~5% of chest pain presentations to the Emergency Department.  
- It's a common distractor for acute coronary syndrome (STEMI mimicker).  
- Complications (tamponade, constriction) are life-threatening but treatable if caught early.  
- Management differs from MI: giving anticoagulation can be disastrous (causes haemorrhagic effusion).

### 3. Pathophysiology — The "Why" Behind The Findings
The pericardium has two layers: a visceral layer adherent to the heart and a parietal layer forming the outer sac. A small amount of serous fluid allows frictionless movement. When inflamed (by infection, autoimmunity, or ischaemia), both layers become hyperaemic and coated with fibrin—rough, like sandpaper.

- **Pain mechanism:** The parietal layer (especially the lower portion) is innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3-C5). Inflammation activates nociceptors, causing sharp pain referred to the shoulder tip (trapezius ridge) and sometimes to the neck or left arm. The pain is worse when the heart moves against the inflamed layers—supine position increases contact, while leaning forward reduces it.  
- **Pericardial rub:** Friction between the two roughened surfaces produces a scratchy, high-pitched sound, often triphasic (atrial systole, ventricular systole, ventricular diastole). It's best heard with the diaphragm of the stethoscope at the left lower sternal edge, with the patient leaning forward and holding expiration.  
- **ECG changes:** Inflammation of the epicardium (subepicardial myocardium) causes a current of injury that is global (diffuse ST elevation) rather than localised to a coronary territory. PR segment depression occurs because atrial epicardium is also inflamed (atrial current of injury). These changes evolve over days (four stages): Stage I – diffuse ST elevation + PR depression; Stage II – ST normalises, T waves flatten; Stage III – T wave inversion; Stage IV – normalisation.  
- **Effusion:** Increased capillary permeability allows protein-rich fluid to leak into the pericardial space. Small amounts (<100 mL) are asymptomatic; rapid accumulation (>200 mL) can compress the right ventricle, causing tamponade. Slow accumulation (e.g., TB or malignancy) can tolerate >1 L before symptoms appear.  
- **Constrictive physiology:** Chronic inflammation leads to fibrotic thickening and calcification of the pericardium, restricting diastolic filling. This causes equalisation of diastolic pressures in all four chambers, leading to Kussmaul sign (increased JVP on inspiration) and a "dip-and-plateau" waveform on cardiac catheterisation.

Now link back to pattern recognition: the positional pain and rub arise directly from friction; the ECG changes reflect global subepicardial inflammation; the recent URI clues a viral trigger that incites the inflammatory cascade.

### 4. Causes / Triggers (organised in bins)
| Category | Common examples | Stem clue that points to it |
|----------|----------------|-----------------------------|
| **Idiopathic** | (~50% of cases) | No trigger identified; classic presentation in healthy young adult |
| **Infectious** | Viral: Coxsackie, Echo, Influenza, EBV, CMV | Recent URI, fever, myalgias (viral prodrome) |
| | Bacterial: *Staph aureus*, *TB* | Immunocompromised, HIV, TB contacts, septic picture |
| | Fungal: *Histoplasma*, *Candida* | Steroid use, transplant, travel to endemic areas |
| **Autoimmune / Inflammatory** | SLE, Rheumatoid Arthritis, Scleroderma, Sarcoidosis | Known rheumatologic disease, malar rash, joint pain, positive ANA/RF |
| **Post-cardiac injury** | Post-MI (Dressler’s), post-pericardiotomy, post-PCI | Recent MI (1-4 weeks earlier), cardiac surgery, PCI within weeks |
| **Uraemic** | Renal failure on dialysis | Cr >600, BUN >60, patient is oliguric or on HD |
| **Neoplastic** | Lung, breast, lymphoma, leukaemia, melanoma | Known malignancy, large effusion with tamponade, haemorrhagic effusion |
| **Radiation-induced** | Mediastinal radiation (for Hodgkin’s, breast) | History of radiation 5-10 years prior; often constrictive |
| **Drug-induced** | Procainamide, hydralazine, isoniazid, minoxidil | Use of these drugs (procainamide is classic for SLE-like syndrome) |

**Most common cause:** Idiopathic (presumed viral). In practice, 80-90% of acute cases are self-limited viral or idiopathic.

### 5. Investigations — What, Why, What It Means
**Diagnostic criteria:** The European Society of Cardiology (ESC) 2015 guidelines require at least **two** of the following:
1. Chest pain (sharp, pleuritic, positional)
2. Pericardial rub
3. ECG changes (diffuse ST elevation or PR depression)
4. New or worsening pericardial effusion

| Test | What it shows | Why you order it | Interpretation |
|------|---------------|------------------|----------------|
| **ECG** | Diffuse concave-up ST elevation (anterior, inferior, lateral) with PR depression lead II, V5, V6; PR elevation in aVR. Stage I pattern. | Confirm pericarditis; differentiate from STEMI. | STEMI is focal (convex ST elevation), reciprocal ST depression, Q waves. Pericarditis does not have Q waves. |
| **Troponin** | Mild elevation (<5x normal) in ~30% due to myopericarditis. | Differentiate from MI. | High troponin (>10x) suggests MI or myopericarditis with significant myocardial involvement. |
| **CRP / ESR** | Markedly raised (CRP often >50 mg/L). | Supports active inflammation; follow to monitor treatment response. | Falling CRP indicates response to NSAIDs/colchicine. |
| **Echocardiogram** | An echo-free space between pericardium and epicardium (effusion). Assess haemodynamic significance (right atrial/ventricular collapse). | All patients: rule out effusion, tamponade, constrictive physiology. | Small effusion (<1 cm) benign; moderate (1-2 cm) watch; large (>2 cm) caution. Diastolic collapse of RV = tamponade. |
| **Chest X-ray** | "Water-bottle" silhouette if large effusion (>300 mL). | Rule out pneumonia (pleuritic pain mimic), assess heart size. | Normal CXR does not exclude pericarditis. |
| **Viral PCR / serology** | Positive for Coxsackie, EBV, CMV, etc. | Only if recurrent or atypical course. | Rarely changes management. |

**Pattern-recognition: what single finding confirms?**
- A **pericardial rub** is pathognomonic for acute pericarditis when heard—if present, stop thinking of MI.
- **Diffuse ST elevation + PR depression** is highly specific (but rub alone is more specific; ECG alone can still be normal in ~20%).

**What single finding rules out?**
- Normal CRP and ESR with chest pain makes acute pericarditis very unlikely (inflammatory marker negative). However, early (<12 hours) CRP may be normal.

### 6. Management — Approach & Reasoning
**Immediate (first hour)**
- **Rest and analgesia:** Sit the patient upright. Give:
  - Ibuprofen 600-800 mg **or** Aspirin 750-1000 mg **or** Indomethacin 50 mg PO (all TDS with food). NSAIDs are first-line because they reduce inflammation quickly.
  - Colchicine 0.5 mg (if <70 kg) or 0.5 mg BD (if ≥70 kg) PO.
  - Colchicine starting on Day 1 reduces recurrence rates from 40% to 15%.
- **Why:** Block COX-2 to lower prostaglandin-induced inflammation. Colchicine inhibits microtubule polymerisation, reducing neutrophilic infiltration.
- **What NOT to do:** Do NOT give glucocorticoids as first-line – they are associated with higher recurrence rates and can mask infection. Only use if refractory, NSAID-allergic, or specific aetiologies (e.g., SLE, uremia). If used, give prednisolone 0.2-0.5 mg/kg/day tapered over weeks.

**Ward (24-72 hours)**
- Monitor daily: pain, vital signs, ECG, CRP. Look for effusion on echo.
- Continue NSAIDs + colchicine. Goal: pain-free for 24-48 hours before tapering NSAIDs (over 2-4 weeks).
- Treat underlying cause if identified:
  - Viral: supportive
  - Bacterial: IV antibiotics (± drainage)
  - TB: quadruple anti-TB therapy
  - Uremic: intensify dialysis (heparin-free)
- **Discharge criteria:** Afebrile, pain-free, CRP falling, effusion small/stable, no haemodynamic compromise.

**Long-term**
- Continue colchicine for **3 months** (full course reduces recurrence). Watch for diarrhoea, myelosuppression (dose adjust in renal impairment, CrCl <30 → halve dose).
- Advise against strenuous exercise until asymptomatic and CRP normal (ongoing inflammation predisposes to recurrence).
- Manage recurrent pericarditis: repeat course, consider steroid-sparing agents (anakinra, azathioprine) under specialist supervision.

**Common pitfalls to avoid:**
- ❌ Starting steroids too early (increases recurrences)
- ❌ Missing a small effusion that becomes tamponade after discharge
- ❌ Prescribing anticoagulants for suspected PE while the patient actually has pericarditis (risk of haemopericardium)
- ❌ Using high-dose NSAIDs in elderly with renal impairment (check eGFR first)

### 7. Differential Diagnosis — Comparison Table
| Feature | Pericarditis | Acute MI (STEMI) | Pulmonary Embolism | Costochondritis |
|---------|--------------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| **Pain** | Sharp, pleuritic, positional (better leaning forward) | Crushing, heavy, non-pleuritic, radiates to jaw/arm | Pleuritic, sharp, with dyspnoea, sometimes haemoptysis | Localised to costochondral junctions, reproducible on palpation |
| **Exam** | Pericardial rub (scratchy), may have mild fever, tachycardia | Often normal; signs of LV failure, S4 gallop | Tachypnoea, tachycardia, signs of DVT, loud P2 | Point tenderness at rib-sternal joints; no rub |
| **ECG** | Diffuse concave ST elevation, PR depression, no Q waves | Focal convex ST elevation, reciprocal ST depression, new Q waves | S1Q3T3 (rare), RV strain (right axis, RBBB) | Normal |
| **Labs** | CRP/ESR very high, troponin mildly elevated | Troponin markedly elevated (>10x), CRP normal or late rise | D-dimer high, troponin often normal or mildly elevated (RV strain) | Normal |
| **Key diff** | Rub pathognomonic; PR depression on ECG | Coronary angiography shows occlusion; troponin curve | CT pulmonary angiogram shows clot; D-dimer positive | Tenderness on palpation alone; normal ECG and labs |

### 8. Complications — What If You Miss It?
**1. Cardiac Tamponade (life-threatening)**
- **How it presents:** Hours to days after symptom onset. Patient develops dyspnoea, hypotension, cold peripheries, elevated JVP (Beck’s triad: hypotension + muffled heart sounds + JVD). Pulsus paradoxus (drop in systolic BP >10 mmHg on inspiration).
- **Key signs:** Kussmaul sign may be absent; electrical alternans on ECG (beat-to-beat variation in QRS amplitude).
- **What to do:**
  - Call senior + ICU immediately
  - Urgent bedside echo: diastolic collapse of right atrium/ventricle
  - Emergency pericardiocentesis: subxiphoid approach, drain >100 mL to restore BP
  - Can give IV fluids 500-1000 mL while preparing drainage (to preload the compressed ventricle)

**2. Constrictive Pericarditis (subacute/chronic)**
- **How it presents:** Weeks to months after acute pericarditis. Patient develops chronic right heart failure: ascites, peripheral oedema, hepatomegaly, elevated JVP with Kussmaul sign (inspiratory JVP rise). Exertional dyspnoea, fatigue.
- **Key signs:** Pericardial knock (early diastolic sound), JVP rises during inspiration, abdominal distension out of proportion to peripheral oedema.
- **What to do:**
  - Cross-sectional imaging (CT/MRI) to assess pericardial thickness >4 mm, calcification
  - Cardiac catheterisation: dip-and-plateau (square root sign)
  - Medical management: diuretics, reduce preload
  - Definitive: pericardiectomy for haemodynamically significant constriction

**3. Recurrent Pericarditis (most common late complication)**
- **How it presents:** After initial resolution, patient has repeated episodes of chest pain, fever, CRP rises. Can occur weeks to years later.
- **Key patterns:** Classic if not on colchicine; treat with full course again; consider steroid-sparing agents (anakinra) if refractory.

**Late-presentation patterns in exams:**
- "A 60-year-old man with history of viral pericarditis 6 months ago now presents with ascites and pedal oedema. JVP is 12 cm, Kussmaul sign positive. What is the diagnosis?" → Constrictive pericarditis.
- "A 45-year-old woman on haemodialysis has chest pain and CT shows a 3 cm pericardial effusion. She is normotensive but has pulsus paradoxus. ECG shows low voltage and electrical alternans." → Uraemic pericarditis with early tamponade.

### 9. High-Yield Mnemonics & Pearls
**Mnemonics:**
- **VISIT PUD** – Causes of pericarditis: Viral, Idiopathic, SLE, Infarction, Trauma, Post-op, Uremic, Drugs (procainamide, hydralazine)
- **PQRST Pain** – Positional, Quality (sharp), Radiation (trapezius), Severity, Timing (sudden onset)
- **ABC of Pericarditis** – Avoid anticoagulants, Bed rest & NSAIDs, Colchicine

**Clinical Pearls (pattern-recognition cues):**
1. **"Leaning forward"** – The one question that changes everything: if pain improves leaning forward, think pericarditis until proven otherwise.
2. **PR depression** – This is the most under-recognised ECG sign; look for it in aVR (PR elevation) and lead II (PR depression). Very specific.
3. **Concave ST elevation** – "Happy face" or "smiley face" shape (concave upward) versus the convex "tombstone" of STEMI.
4. **Troponin** – Mild rise does NOT rule out pericarditis; it suggests myopericarditis but same treatment.
5. **Echo is not optional** – Even if no rub, small effusions can be present; always check.
6. **CRP as guide** – Stop NSAIDs only when CRP normalises, even if pain resolves earlier (treat the inflammation).
7. **Recurrence prevention** – Colchicine 3 months is the most important intervention; failure to prescribe it is a common mistake.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Giving steroids for mild pericarditis → recurrence rate triples.
- Missing tamponade because you didn't check pulsus paradoxus or bedside echo.
- Confusing pericarditis with STEMI and giving heparin → catastrophic haemopericardium.
- Not checking renal function before colchicine → toxicity (especially in elderly, CKD).

### 10. Escalation Criteria
**When to call your senior / on-call team immediately:**

- **Haemodynamic instability:** SBP <90 mmHg despite fluids, or MAP <65, or sustained tachycardia >120 bpm
- **Signs of tamponade:** Beck’s triad (hypotension, muffled heart sounds, elevated JVP), pulsus paradoxus >20 mmHg, electrical alternans on ECG
- **NEWS2 ≥5** (especially respiratory rate >24, SpO2 <92%, altered consciousness) – potential early tamponade or sepsis
- **Lactate >2.5 mmol/L** – marker of low cardiac output
- **GCS <15**, confusion, or agitation – may indicate cerebral hypoperfusion from tamponade
- **Echo finding of moderate-large effusion (>2 cm)** with right atrial or ventricular collapse (even if BP normal) – needs urgent drainage
- **Failure to improve after 48 hours** with high CRP persisting despite NSAIDs + colchicine – consider atypical cause (TB, neoplastic)

**When to call ICU / crash team:**  
- Cardiac arrest (pulseless electrical activity – PEA – often due to tamponade)
- Profound hypotension (SBP <70) with loss of consciousness
- The patient is being prepared for emergency pericardiocentesis → you need an experienced proceduralist; if not available, transfer to ICU/cardiac catheter lab immediately

*Remember: pericardial tamponade is one of the few reversible causes of PEA arrest. Immediate drainage can be life-saving.*
